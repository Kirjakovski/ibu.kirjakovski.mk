LECTURE 1: Introduction to Cognitive Psychology

Date: 01 Oct 2025
Cognitive Process

Summary:

Cognitive psychology is the scientific study of mental processes, viewing the mind as a system that creates representations of the world to control functions like perception, attention, memory, and reasoning. The field’s roots are in philosophy, with thinkers like René Descartes proposing mind-body dualism and John Locke suggesting the mind is a “tabula rasa,” shaped by experience.

The scientific study began with Wilhelm Wundt’s structuralism, which aimed to identify the basic elements of consciousness through analytic introspection. This was followed by William James’s functionalism, which focused on how the mind helps individuals adapt to their environment. Gestalt psychology, founded by Max Wertheimer, shifted focus to how the mind organizes perceptions into meaningful wholes, asserting that the whole is more than the sum of its parts. However, the rise of behaviorism, championed by John B. Watson and B. F. Skinner, led to a period where psychology abandoned the study of the mind in favor of observable stimulus-response behaviors, analyzed through classical and operant conditioning.

The “cognitive revolution” marked a return to studying internal mental processes. This shift was fueled by evidence that behaviorism couldn’t fully explain certain behaviors, such as rats forming “cognitive maps” of mazes. The advent of the digital computer provided a powerful new metaphor for the mind as an information-processing system. Pioneers like Donders measured the time it takes to make a decision, while Ebbinghaus quantitatively studied memory and forgetting. Ulric Neisser’s 1967 book formally defined the field. Modern cognitive psychology integrates neuroscience, using tools like fMRI and EEG to observe brain activity during cognitive tasks, and explores concepts like neural networks.
