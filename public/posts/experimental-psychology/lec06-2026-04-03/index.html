#06: Formulating a Hypothesis

EXPERIMENTAL PSYCHOLOGY
03 Apr 2026

Lecture Summary:

A hypothesis serves as the formal predictive idea of an experiment, acting as a specific and measurable bridge between at least two variables. It is the end product of a rigorous thinking process rather than a simple hunch. In research, hypotheses are divided into experimental and nonexperimental types. Nonexperimental designs focus on describing potential correlations or relationships without claiming direct cause and effect. In contrast, experimental hypotheses use conditional logic to predict exactly how specific antecedent conditions will influence measured behavior, allowing researchers to make robust causal claims through the manipulation of independent variables.

To be scientifically valid, a hypothesis must meet five evaluative criteria: it must be synthetic, testable, falsifiable, parsimonious, and fruitful. A synthetic statement is one that can be proven either true or false through “If… then” logic, avoiding statements that are always true or inherently contradictory. Testability requires the current technological capacity to manipulate and measure variables, while falsifiability ensures that negative results are accepted as evidence against the prediction. Parsimony favors the simplest explanation with the fewest assumptions, and fruitfulness describes the ability of a hypothesis to stimulate further research and new studies.

Research problems typically originate from four main sources: everyday life observations, practical operational challenges, gaps in past research, and established theories. These problems are investigated using two logical models. The inductive model builds general principles from specific observations, while the deductive model derives specific predictions from an overarching general theory. Most mature scientific disciplines unite these into a continuous cycle of observation and testing. Occasionally, valuable discoveries occur through serendipity, where researchers analytically pursue unexpected anomalies. Regardless of whether an idea comes from deep theory or expert intuition, it must be stated as a clear question about the relationship between variables that can be physically observed and tested.
