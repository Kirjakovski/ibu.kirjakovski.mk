#03: Social Cognition

SOCIAL PSYCHOLOGY
27 Feb 2026

Lecture Summary:

Social cognition serves as the intricate framework through which individuals process, store, and apply information about others and the surrounding social environment. This cognitive journey begins with automatic thinking, a non-conscious and effortless mechanism that utilizes mental structures known as schemas to organize knowledge and fill gaps in situational understanding. These schemas are highly accessible due to past experiences, current goals, or recent priming, often influencing perceptions so profoundly that they can lead to self-fulfilling prophecies. In such instances, an individual’s expectations of another person can inadvertently shape their own behavior, which then elicits a response that confirms the original, perhaps erroneous, belief.

Beyond these involuntary processes, the mind employs various mental shortcuts, or judgmental heuristics, to make efficient decisions without exhaustive research. The availability heuristic, for example, leads people to base judgments on how easily specific instances come to mind, while the representativeness heuristic involves classifying things based on their similarity to a typical case. These cognitive patterns are not universal; they are deeply influenced by cultural determinants. Western cultures typically favor an analytic thinking style that focuses on the individual properties of objects, whereas East Asian cultures often adopt a holistic approach, emphasizing the broader context and the relationships between various elements within a social scene.

In contrast to the rapid-fire nature of automatic pilot, controlled thinking represents a conscious, intentional, and effortful mode of social perception. This high-effort processing allows for counterfactual reasoning—the mental act of undoing the past to imagine “what might have been”—and provides a critical check against the overconfidence barrier that often plagues human judgment. When interpreting the behavior of others, individuals typically navigate a two-step attribution process: initially making an internal, dispositional assumption before potentially adjusting for situational factors. By integrating nonverbal communication cues such as facial expressions and body language with deliberate statistical reasoning, we refine our ability to form accurate first impressions and navigate the complexities of human interaction.
