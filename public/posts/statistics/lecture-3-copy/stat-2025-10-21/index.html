LECTURE 3: Basics of Inferential Statistics

Date: 21 Oct 2025
Statistics

Summary:

This lecture introduces inferential statistics, the procedures used for drawing conclusions that extend beyond the specific scores collected in a study. It establishes the fundamental distinction between a population (the entire group of interest) and a sample (the particular group of people studied). Research involves studying a sample and using its sample statistics (like the mean, M, and standard deviation, SD) to make inferences about the unknown population parameters (like μ and σ) . A central tool for this is the normal curve, which is a specific, mathematically defined, bell-shaped, symmetrical, and unimodal frequency distribution. Its importance is highlighted by the Central Limit Theorem (CLT). The CLT states that as sample size increases, the distribution of sample means will approach a normal distribution, regardless of the original population’s shape.

To understand a score’s position within this distribution, the Z score is used. A Z score indicates the number of standard deviations a raw score is above or below the mean , and it is calculated using the formula Z=(X−M)/SD. A distribution of Z scores always has a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1.

The standard nature of the normal curve means there is a known percentage of scores between any two points. For example, approximately 34% of scores fall between the mean and 1 SD , and 95% of all data falls between Z scores of -1.96 and +1.96. This allows for the calculation of probability , or the expected relative frequency of an outcome. Using a normal curve table or Excel functions , it is possible to determine the percentage of scores above or below a specific value, such as the percentage of people with an IQ above 125.
