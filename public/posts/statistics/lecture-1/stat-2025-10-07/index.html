LECTURE 1: Measurement, Data, Distributions, Tables, and Graphs

Date: 07 Oct 2025
Statistics

Summary:

Statistics is a branch of mathematics focused on organizing, analyzing, and interpreting numerical data, primarily divided into two main areas. Descriptive statistics are used to summarize and make sense of a group of scores, while inferential statistics allow for drawing broader conclusions from the data collected in a study. Understanding statistics is crucial for conducting effective research, comprehending scientific literature, and developing critical thinking skills.

The foundation of statistical analysis begins with data, which are observations or measurements. A characteristic that can have different values is known as a variable, and a specific person’s value on that variable is a score. These variables can be measured on different scales, or levels of measurement. Nominal variables use categories or names, such as gender or ethnicity. Ordinal variables represent a rank order, like competition placements. Interval variables have equal intervals between values but lack a true zero, such as temperature in Celsius. Ratio variables possess all the properties of interval scales plus a true zero point, including measures like age, height, and income.

To make sense of raw data, it is often organized into frequency tables, which list how many times each value appears. For data with many values, a grouped frequency table, which organizes scores into intervals, is more practical. This information can then be visually represented through graphs. A common type is the histogram, a bar graph that displays the frequency of values, helping to visualize the shape of the data’s distribution.

Frequency distributions reveal patterns in the data. They can be unimodal (one peak), bimodal (two peaks), or symmetrical. A common symmetrical, unimodal distribution is the bell-shaped normal curve. Distributions can also be skewed, where scores pile up on one side. This can sometimes be caused by floor effects, where scores cluster at the low end, or ceiling effects, where they cluster at the high end. It is also important to be aware of how graphs can be constructed in misleading ways, for instance, by altering the scale of an axis to exaggerate differences.
