LECTURE 7: Comparing Groups – ANOVA

Date: 15 Dec 2025
Advanced Quantitative Research Methods

Summary:

The lecture provides a comprehensive overview of Analysis of Variance (ANOVA), a statistical procedure utilized to test variations among the means of three or more groups. While independent means t-tests are suitable for comparing two groups, ANOVA is specifically designed to handle the complexity of multiple groups simultaneously, thereby avoiding the redundancy of multiple t-tests. One-way ANOVA focuses on groups differing across a single independent variable, whereas factorial ANOVA examines situations involving more than one dimension.

The basic logic of ANOVA involves comparing two different estimates of population variance: within-groups variance and between-groups variance. The within-groups estimate reflects variation caused solely by chance factors, such as individual differences or measurement error, and remains independent of the null hypothesis. The between-groups estimate reflects both chance variation and potential treatment effects. If the null hypothesis is true and the population means are equal, these two estimates should be approximately equal, resulting in an F ratio of approximately 1. However, if the research hypothesis is true, the between-groups estimate will be significantly larger than the within-groups estimate, yielding an F ratio greater than 1.

The F distribution is a positively skewed curve used to determine significance, with its exact shape depending on the degrees of freedom for both the numerator (between-groups) and denominator (within-groups). In a fictional study regarding criminal records and jury perceptions, the calculated F ratio of 4.07 exceeded the critical cutoff of 3.89 for a .05 significance level, leading to the rejection of the null hypothesis.

Beyond the omnibus F test, researchers may use planned contrasts for a priori comparisons or post hoc tests, such as the Bonferroni procedure or Scheffé test, to identify specific differences between means while controlling for Type I error. Additionally, factorial research designs allow for the examination of main effects and interaction effects, where the impact of one grouping variable depends on the level of another.
