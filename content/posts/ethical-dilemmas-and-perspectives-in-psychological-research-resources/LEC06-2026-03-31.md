## Lecture Summary:

The structural foundation of psychological research depends on the "ethical calculus equilibrium". This means that the broad scientific gains and the advancement of knowledge must be more important than the specific burdens placed on participants, such as psychological discomfort or time investment. Researchers have an "affirmative ethical obligation" called beneficence, which is the active pursuit of creating constructive value for society. At the same time, the principle of nonmaleficence requires a "preventative mandate" to shield human subjects from harm, exploitation, and systemic distress.

To protect participants, investigators must evaluate many different "risk vectors," including physical pain, acute cognitive stress, and social stigmatization. If unexpected physical or psychological harm appears, "study stopping rules" must be used to immediately end all experimental procedures. These non-negotiable triggers are more important than scientific objectives or funding. Additionally, researchers must use "rigorous anonymization algorithms" to permanently separate a person's identity from the raw data. This helps prevent "structural anonymity failures" that could lead to negative consequences like job loss or legal liability.

Using flawed methods is considered a severe ethical violation because it wastes participant time and societal funding without producing valid data. Research integrity also requires "unwavering intellectual honesty" to avoid the most serious types of misconduct: fabrication (making up data), falsification (changing real data), and plagiarism (stealing others' work). While there might be different risk thresholds when investigating powerful or corrupt institutions, all researchers must follow a "universal mandate" to avoid cultural malpractice. Ultimately, the goal is to protect "empirical truth" and maintain public trust in science.
